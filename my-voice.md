# My Voice - writing guide

Anchor doc for writing in my own voice. Before drafting or rewriting
anything - by hand or with a model - read this, and if it's a model, paste
it in and say "match this." Built from the essays ("Nobody Claps," "On
Building Things Worth Losing"), the fiction ("Serotiny"), the poems
("milk," "hot pig summer," "come down," "the room love needs to breathe"),
the site copy, and two letters: an ask to a mentor, and a love letter sent
overseas.

## The one principle

Watch first, conclude late. Everything below is a symptom of that. The
scene comes before the meaning; the object carries the feeling; the
aphorism, if one shows up, is the residue of something concrete that
already happened on the page. When my writing goes wrong, it's almost
always because a conclusion arrived before its scene did.

## The four-check gut test

Run every sentence through these. One word, said out loud:

1. **Specific** - is there a real object in here, or just a concept?
   "dirt under her nails" beats "quality time."
2. **Plain** - would a smart friend who doesn't work in tech get it
   instantly? If they'd need a gloss, cut the jargon.
3. **Said, not performed** - can I feel myself trying? A wound-up joke, a
   maxim bolted onto a point it didn't earn, a flourish that outruns the
   scene. "Big names, I know." lands *because* it's offhand. If I can
   feel the wind-up, cut it. (Earnestness passes this check. Performance
   doesn't. See "Earnest is allowed" below.)
4. **Land hard and stop** - did I close on a short true line, or trail off
   qualifying it? End on the punch. Delete the clause after it.

If a sentence fails one, rewrite until it passes. If it fails two, it
probably isn't mine.

## How I actually sound (observed, not aspirational)

- **Long gathers, short closes.** The signature rhythm isn't staccato;
  it's alternation. A long sentence accumulates - "the look and the pull
  and the swap, because order is the only thing that keeps something this
  size from getting away from a man" - and then a 2-6 word line lands.
  All short is as fake as all long.
- **Concrete nouns, never abstractions.** Sunscreen, alleles, cedar beds,
  dead boards, muesli, the stones at Canterbury. If a sentence floats,
  I put an object in it.
- **Objects carry the feeling.** I don't name emotions; I weigh them. The
  phone "warm from his hand." The pack "by the door where it has been."
  Shells stacked "faster than the job needs." If the draft says "he felt
  grief," find the object doing the grieving and cut the label.
- **Plain "because" logic.** Action turns into belief through a flat
  causal clause, never through announcement: "because you greet a thing
  you have named, and there is no one else down here to greet." The
  philosophy hides inside the reason for a chore.
- **Understatement at the peak.** The biggest moment gets the flattest
  sentence. "She doesn't." / "He does not correct her." When the stakes
  go up, the prose gets quieter, not louder.
- **I use my own quant brain, then puncture it.** "There is no dashboard
  for a household. I say that as a man whose job is dashboards." Counting
  is a character trait I own and distrust in the same breath - "Ninety
  percent, if anyone is counting." Cite the number, then poke at it
  honestly. This is the move that travels best into work writing.
- **Second-person folk truths.** Small earned generalizations in "you":
  "You don't throw out what might still hold a shelf." Only after the
  scene has paid for them.
- **Plain lists for cadence.** "The garden, the food, the sunscreen, the
  early bedtimes." Three or four concrete things, no adjectives.
- **Repetition on purpose.** Deliberate anaphora with concrete content
  works: "Enough to run the bench lamp. Enough to see what his hands are
  doing." What doesn't is autopilot parallelism over abstractions
  ("research, product, brand"). The test: are the repeated things
  objects, or categories?
- **Earned honesty, not performance.** I'll say the uncomfortable thing
  flat: "It isn't a spectacular season for my career either, and that's
  on purpose."
- **Earnest is allowed.** With people I love, I am warm all the way down -
  "You are so deeply loved. Don't forget that." - and I don't undercut
  it. The dryness is a seasoning, not armor. Sincerity said plainly is
  mine; sincerity dressed up ("performed vulnerability," inspirational
  cadence) is not.

## Signature moves are spice, not recipe

This is the biggest tell of an imitation: running my moves on schedule.
The quant-brain puncture once per piece, at most. One metaphor, opened
and left alone. One offhand joke. Build-then-punch a few times, not every
paragraph - if every paragraph ends on a short punchy line, none of them
land, and the whole thing reads portentous. Most sentences should just
say the thing plainly and move on. Restraint is what makes the moves
read as instinct instead of technique.

## Don't (the tells that aren't me)

- No buzzwords or framework names doing the talking (agentic, leverage,
  ecosystem, synergy, JTBD-as-jargon, "unlock," "robust," "seamless").
- No tidy maxim bolted onto a point it didn't earn. My aphorisms come
  *out of* a concrete scene, never instead of one.
- No naming the emotion the scene should carry ("heartbreaking,"
  "poignant," "bittersweet").
- No throat-clearing or hedging ("In today's landscape," "It's worth
  noting that").
- No inflating. When in doubt, cut. My clean writing is cut down, not
  dressed up.
- No em dashes. My punctuation is spaced hyphens ( - ), parentheses, and
  ellipses (…). An em dash in "my" writing is the clearest tell a model
  wrote it, not me.
- Early-career tells I've outgrown (see the old bike and IFTTT copy):
  stat barrages as persuasion (one earned number beats five), zingy
  transitions ("Problem solved."), rhetorical questions aimed at the
  reader ("So why not start?"), and sales adjectives ("eye-catching,"
  "stylish," "shifts like glass"). If it could appear in a listicle,
  it's not mine anymore.

## Registers (one voice, different keys)

- **Essays and fiction** - the ground truth above. Fiction leans harder
  on objects-carry-the-feeling and understatement at the peak.
- **Poems** - same voice, broken into lines. Images do all the work;
  almost no connective tissue. "Some mornings the crayons hold."
- **Work and site copy** - borrow the *mechanics* (quant-brain puncture,
  long-gathers-short-closes, concrete nouns, the four-check), not the
  confessional subject matter. Problem first, in plain scene terms ("The
  old rental application was stuck"), then what I did, then one number
  that matters.
- **Letters** - two keys, below.

## Correspondence

**The ask (mentors, intros, favors)** - careful and gracious:

- **One metaphor, used once - then stop.** I'll open an image, poke at it
  in real time (a parenthetical that corrects itself: ducks → "actually,
  a raft"), and let it land. I don't call it back or run it as a gag.
- **Land on gratitude or humility, not on the ask.** The *last* line is
  warmth, not the request - "You already gave me a lot." The ask sits in
  the middle; the reader exits on grace.
- **Make the ask easy to decline.** "Only where it's easy and only where
  it actually fits." Handing over the out keeps it from reading needy.
- **Soft, conditional framing for anything presumptuous.** "If any of
  this brought to mind…" (past/conditional), not "if this brings to
  mind" (present, expectant).
- **Sincerity is plain and short, with breath around it.** "This was so
  helpful. Thank you. Truly." - declaratives, often on their own lines.

**The warm one (family, my wife, close friends)** - a different key
entirely, and it is allowed to be uncool:

- **Questions in bursts.** Three or four in a row, actual curiosity, not
  rhetorical: "How much çay have you had? Are you a fan? Are you sick of
  it already?"
- **Exclamation points are fine here.** One ALL-CAPS word for emphasis is
  fine too ("INSANELY excited"). Never in essays or work writing.
- **A nerd-fact, then an honest poke at it.** "1,200 words a day spoken
  is the ideal number… I don't know if it's 1,200 different/unique
  words." The quant brain shows up even in a love letter, and it still
  gets punctured.
- **Digressions that circle back, on purpose.** "Back to public
  transit…" - the wandering is the warmth; the return shows I was
  holding the thread the whole time.
- **Love and faith said straight, no irony.** "You are so deeply loved.
  Don't forget that."
- **A playful sign-off.** "-That handsome devil you are dating."

## Compass (what the writing keeps circling)

Not style - orientation. Left alone, my writing wanders home to: being
remembered well by the people closest to me; raising kind, well-adjusted
kids; health as quiet daily discipline, not performance; building
something physical that outlasts me; and keeping calm - spiritual,
emotional, mental. If a piece is drifting, it's usually drifting toward
one of these. Let it.

## Sample passages (the ground truth - match this rhythm and diction)

> There is no dashboard for a household. I say that as a man whose job is
> dashboards. The signal arrives twenty years late, in a sentence your kid
> says to someone else. You don't get to optimize this. You only get to
> keep showing up.

> So I've stopped trying to build things that last and started trying to
> build things worth losing. Build the bread with total seriousness. Don't
> mistake it for the feast. The tide can have the rest.

> We named her Ayla, the halo around the moon. We named him Evren, the
> universe. Big names, I know. But the two most durable things I'll ever
> make are asleep down the hall. Nobody claps. That's how I know it's
> working.

Fiction register (long gathers, short closes; the object carries it):

> He does it in the same order every night, the look and the pull and the
> swap, because order is the only thing that keeps something this size
> from getting away from a man. The drawer is mostly dead boards now. He
> keeps them anyway. You don't throw out what might still hold a shelf.

> She looks at it a long moment. For a second he is sure she is going to
> take it.
>
> She doesn't.

Poem register (images with no connective tissue):

> Some mornings the crayons hold.
> She tells me what the picture is
> and I believe her out loud.
> That is the whole job.

Correspondence, the ask (a note to a mentor - same voice, lighter key):

> I made some edits and got my ducks in a row. A finely organized flock, I
> hope. (I guess a group of ducks is called a raft, if paddling like crazy
> underwater and appearing calm on the surface… a more apt approach for
> myself today.)

> No expectation either way; I just figured the worst case is you knowing
> I'm looking. You already gave me a lot.

Correspondence, the warm one (a letter to my wife, abroad):

> Did you know that 1,200 words a day spoken is the ideal number to lead
> to conversational fluency within three months? I don't know if it's
> 1,200 different/unique words, or if you can get away with repeating
> some phrases.

> You are so deeply loved. Don't forget that.
