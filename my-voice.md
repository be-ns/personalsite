# My Voice — writing guide

Anchor doc for writing in my own voice. Before drafting or rewriting
anything — by hand or with a model — read this, and if it's a model, paste
it in and say "match this." Built from my two essays ("Nobody Claps" and
"On Building Things Worth Losing").

## The four-check gut test

Run every sentence through these. One word, said out loud:

1. **Specific** — is there a real object in here, or just a concept?
   "dirt under her nails" beats "quality time."
2. **Plain** — would a smart friend who doesn't work in tech get it
   instantly? If they'd need a gloss, cut the jargon.
3. **A little wry** — is there one ounce of dryness, or is it earnest all
   the way down? "Big names, I know." earns the sentence around it.
4. **Land hard and stop** — did I close on a short true line, or trail off
   qualifying it? End on the punch. Delete the clause after it.

If a sentence fails one, rewrite until it passes. If it fails two, it
probably isn't mine.

## How I actually sound (observed, not aspirational)

- **Short declarative sentences, and I let fragments land.**
  "It's brutal. It's also curriculum." / "Nobody claps. That's how I know
  it's working."
- **Concrete nouns, never abstractions.** Sunscreen, alleles, cedar beds,
  dark circles, gargoyles, the stones at Canterbury. If a sentence floats,
  I put an object in it.
- **I use my own quant brain, then puncture it.** "There is no dashboard
  for a household. I say that as a man whose job is dashboards." The move
  is: invoke the data-guy identity, then admit where it fails. This is the
  one that travels best into work writing.
- **Build, then punch.** A longer sentence does the work; a 2–4 word line
  closes it. Rhythm comes from that contrast, not from fancy words.
- **Plain lists for cadence.** "The garden, the food, the sunscreen, the
  early bedtimes." Three or four concrete things, no adjectives.
- **Earned honesty, not performance.** I'll say the uncomfortable thing
  flat: "It isn't a spectacular season for my career either, and that's
  on purpose."
- **Dry, understated warmth.** Wry, never showy. Optimistic underneath.

## Don't (the tells that aren't me)

- No buzzwords or framework names doing the talking (agentic, leverage,
  ecosystem, synergy, JTBD-as-jargon, "unlock," "robust," "seamless").
- No tidy maxim bolted onto a point it didn't earn. My aphorisms come
  *out of* a concrete scene, never instead of one.
- No rule-of-three parallelism on autopilot ("research, product, brand").
- No throat-clearing or hedging ("In today's landscape," "It's worth
  noting that").
- No inflating. When in doubt, cut. My clean writing is cut down, not
  dressed up.

## One note on range

The essays are personal — kids, mortality, faith. For work writing, borrow
the *mechanics* (the quant-brain puncture, build-then-punch, concrete
nouns, the four-check), not the subject matter. The voice carries; the
confessional doesn't have to.

## Sample passages (the ground truth — match this rhythm and diction)

> There is no dashboard for a household. I say that as a man whose job is
> dashboards. The signal arrives twenty years late, in a sentence your kid
> says to someone else. You don't get to optimize this. You only get to
> keep showing up.

> So I've stopped trying to build things that last and started trying to
> build things worth losing. Build the bread with total seriousness. Don't
> mistake it for the feast. The tide can have the rest.

> My wife told me once she wasn't getting the whole me. That sentence
> reorganized my year. I quit the scroll, the noise, the convenient escape
> hatches, so that when I walk through the door, all of me arrives.

> We named her Ayla, the halo around the moon. We named him Evren, the
> universe. Big names, I know. But the two most durable things I'll ever
> make are asleep down the hall. Nobody claps. That's how I know it's
> working.
